Fine-tuning Embeddings & Re-rankers

When off-the-shelf models hit a ceiling — domain adaptation for retrieval

When to fine-tune

General-purpose embedding models are trained on broad web data. They work well for common topics but struggle with domain-specific vocabulary: medical terminology, legal jargon, internal product codenames, engineering acronyms. If your Recall@10 plateaus below 0.85 despite optimizing chunking and query processing, fine-tuning is the next lever.

Fine-tuning the embedding model

  • Training data: You need (query, positive_passage, negative_passage) triplets. 500-2000 triplets is typically enough for meaningful improvement.
  • Hard negatives: The negative passage should be topically similar but not the correct answer. Use your current retrieval system to find "almost relevant" results — these are the hardest negatives and most informative for training.
  • Loss function: Contrastive loss (InfoNCE) or triplet loss with in-batch negatives. Multiple Negatives Ranking Loss (MNRL) from sentence-transformers is the standard starting point.
  • Expected gains: 10-25% improvement in Recall@10 on domain-specific queries. Minimal impact on general queries.

Fine-tuning the cross-encoder re-ranker

  • Training data: (query, passage, relevance_label) pairs. Binary labels (relevant/not relevant) work, but graded labels (0-3) give better results.
  • Base model: Start with MiniLM or a small DeBERTa model. Fine-tune with cross-entropy loss on your domain's relevance judgments.
  • Data generation: Use your production logs. Queries where users clicked on a result = positive. Queries where users reformulated = the original results were negative.

Generating training data with LLMs

If you don't have production logs yet:

  1. Select 100-200 representative documents from your corpus
  2. Use an LLM to generate 3-5 realistic questions per document
  3. The document is the positive passage; run retrieval to find hard negatives
  4. Have a domain expert review and correct 20% of examples for quality assurance

The exposure bias problem

Your training data only labels what your current system retrieves. Documents that are never retrieved are never labeled — creating a feedback loop that reinforces existing retrieval patterns. Counter this with:

  • Hard negative mining: Deliberately include challenging non-retrieved documents in training
  • Epsilon-greedy exploration: Randomly surface some non-top-ranked results to generate labels from outside the current retrieval distribution

After fine-tuning the embedding model, you must re-embed your entire corpus. This is a full re-indexing operation. Plan for it — on a large corpus, this can take hours and significant compute.
