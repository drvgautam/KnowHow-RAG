Chunking Evaluation

The most neglected evaluation layer — and how to do it right

Why most teams skip it

Chunking feels like a pre-processing step, not a model. Teams treat it as "set and forget" — choose a chunk size, split the documents, move on. But chunking quality directly determines retrieval quality: if the answer is split across two chunks, no amount of re-ranking will fix it.

Three evaluation dimensions

  • Boundary tests: Do chunks split at natural boundaries (paragraphs, sections), or do they cut sentences in half? Sample 100 random chunks and manually check whether each one starts and ends at a coherent boundary.
  • Coherence audits: Can each chunk be understood on its own, without reading the surrounding document? A chunk that says "As mentioned above, the policy allows..." is incoherent in isolation. The pronoun and reference resolution is lost.
  • Table fidelity: Are tables preserved with their headers? Does each table chunk include column names and row context? Tables are the single hardest content type to chunk correctly.

Automated checks

  • Sentence completeness: Flag chunks that start or end mid-sentence (regex for missing capital at start or missing punctuation at end).
  • Orphan reference detection: Flag chunks that start with pronouns ("It", "They", "This") or references ("As noted above") without an antecedent.
  • Size distribution: Plot chunk sizes. A healthy distribution is unimodal. Bimodal distributions suggest the chunker is splitting some document types poorly.

Chunking evaluation must be repeated every time you change the chunking strategy, add a new document type, or update the parsing pipeline. It's not a one-time check.
