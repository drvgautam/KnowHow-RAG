Why Chunking Matters

The highest-leverage decision in your RAG pipeline

What is chunking?

Chunking is the process of splitting documents into smaller pieces that can be individually retrieved. Each chunk becomes a unit of retrieval — when a user asks a question, the system returns the most relevant chunks, not entire documents.

Why chunk size matters

  • Too small (~50 tokens): The chunk lacks context. "The deadline is March 15" without knowing what deadline or which year is useless.
  • Too large (~2000 tokens): The chunk contains too many topics. The embedding becomes a blurry average of unrelated ideas, making it match weakly with any specific query.
  • Right-sized (~200-500 tokens): The chunk covers one coherent idea with enough context to be useful on its own.

Three granularity levels

  • Sentence-level (~100 tokens): Best for precise fact lookups — "What is the deductible for Plan A?"
  • Paragraph-level (~400 tokens): The workhorse size for most questions — enough context for the LLM to generate a complete answer.
  • Section-level (~900 tokens): Used for context expansion — when the top chunk is found, its parent section can be pulled in for surrounding context.

Overlap is essential

Fixed-size chunking without overlap is the most common beginner mistake. Consider a sentence that falls exactly on a chunk boundary:

Chunk 1: "...employees are entitled to"
Chunk 2: "15 days of annual leave..."

Neither chunk alone answers "How many leave days do employees get?" With a 10-20% overlap between chunks, the complete sentence appears in at least one chunk.

Semantic vs. fixed-size chunking

Fixed-size: Split every N tokens. Simple but ignores document structure.

Semantic: Split at natural boundaries — paragraphs, headings, topic shifts. Preserves coherence but requires understanding document structure.

Best practice: use semantic boundaries (headings, paragraphs) as primary split points, with a maximum size limit as a backstop.

Tables are chunking's hardest problem. A table row without its header is meaningless. Always keep table headers attached to every chunk that contains table data.
