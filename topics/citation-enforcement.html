Citation Enforcement

Every claim needs a source — how to make the LLM cite its evidence

Why citations matter

Without citations, the user cannot verify whether the answer actually came from the retrieved documents or was hallucinated. Citations create accountability — both for the system and for the user reviewing the answer.

How to enforce citations

The enforcement happens in the LLM's system prompt and context formatting:

System prompt:
"Answer the user's question using ONLY the
provided context. For every factual claim,
include a citation in the format [Source N].
If the answer is not in the context, say so."

Context format:
[Source 1] HR Policy v3.2, Section 4.1:
"Employees receive 15 days of annual leave..."

[Source 2] Benefits FAQ, Last updated 2024-01:
"Part-time employees receive pro-rated leave..."

What good citations look like

  • Inline: "Employees receive 15 days of annual leave [Source 1], with part-time employees receiving a pro-rated amount [Source 2]."
  • Clickable: In the UI, citations link to the original document or highlight the specific passage.
  • Granular: Each claim has its own citation, not just one citation for the whole answer.

Common failures

  • Phantom citations: The model invents a source number that doesn't exist. Validate citation references against the provided context programmatically.
  • Lazy citations: The model cites a source for the entire answer instead of per-claim. More aggressive prompting and few-shot examples help.
  • Paraphrase drift: The model paraphrases so heavily that the cited source no longer supports the claim. Faithfulness scoring catches this.

Citations serve a dual purpose: they let users verify answers, and they constrain the model's behavior. A model that must cite every claim is less likely to generate unsupported statements because it "knows" it needs evidence for each one.
