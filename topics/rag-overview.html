What is RAG?

Retrieval-Augmented Generation — search first, then generate

The core idea

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is a pattern that combines two capabilities:

  • Retrieval: Find the most relevant documents or passages from a knowledge base given a user's question.
  • Generation: Feed those passages to an LLM and ask it to synthesize a coherent answer grounded in the retrieved evidence.

Neither component works well alone. Retrieval finds evidence but can't synthesize it. Generation synthesizes but hallucinates without evidence. Together, they produce grounded, fluent answers.

How it works (simplified)

  • Step 1: User asks a question: "What's the parental leave policy?"
  • Step 2: The system searches an indexed document corpus and retrieves the top matching passages.
  • Step 3: The retrieved passages are inserted into the LLM's prompt as context.
  • Step 4: The LLM generates an answer using only the provided context, with citations.

Why not just fine-tune the LLM?

Fine-tuning bakes knowledge into model weights. This means:

  • Updating information requires retraining (expensive, slow)
  • You can't easily audit where an answer came from
  • Access control becomes nearly impossible — the model either knows something or it doesn't
  • Fine-tuning teaches style and format well, but is unreliable for memorizing specific facts

RAG keeps knowledge external and retrievable, making it auditable, updatable, and access-controlled.

The two pipelines

A RAG system has two distinct pipelines:

  • Offline pipeline: Processes documents into searchable chunks and indexes them. Runs on a schedule (e.g. weekly).
  • Online pipeline: Handles each user query in real time — retrieves, re-ranks, assembles context, and generates the answer.

The power of RAG is separation of concerns: retrieval quality can be measured independently of generation quality. When the system gives a bad answer, you can trace it to either "wrong documents retrieved" or "right documents, bad synthesis" — and fix the right component.
