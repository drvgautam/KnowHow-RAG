HyDE — Hypothetical Document Embedding

Embed hypothetical answers to improve retrieval

The problem with embedding raw queries

User queries are short and colloquial: "how many vacation days do I get?" But the documents in your corpus are written in formal, structured language: "Employees are entitled to 15 days of paid annual leave per calendar year."

The embedding of the query and the embedding of the answer live in different regions of the vector space because they sound very different, even though they're about the same topic.

The HyDE approach

  • Step 1: Give the user's query to an LLM and ask it to generate a hypothetical answer — a short passage that looks like what a real document might say.
  • Step 2: Embed the hypothetical answer (not the original query).
  • Step 3: Use that embedding to search the document index.

The hypothetical answer is closer in style and vocabulary to the actual documents, so its embedding lands nearer to the real answer in vector space.

Example

User query: "how many vacation days do I get?"

Hypothetical answer (LLM-generated):
"Full-time employees receive 15 days of paid
 annual leave per calendar year, accrued monthly.
 Part-time employees receive a pro-rated amount."

This hypothetical answer is embedded and used
for retrieval instead of the raw question.

When to use HyDE

  • Works well: When queries are short, informal, or use jargon different from the corpus.
  • Works less well: When queries are already document-like, or when the corpus uses the same casual language.
  • Cost: One additional LLM call per query (~200ms with a fast model). Use a small, fast model for this — it doesn't need to be accurate, just plausible.

The hypothetical answer may contain hallucinated facts. This is actually fine — you're only using its embedding to find real documents, not presenting it to the user. The factual content of the hypothetical answer doesn't matter; only its position in embedding space matters.
