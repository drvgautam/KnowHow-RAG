Query Routing & Decomposition

Sending each question to the right pipeline — and breaking complex ones apart

Why one pipeline isn't enough

Not all queries are equal. "What's the WiFi password?" is a simple lookup. "How does our leave policy compare across our three regional offices?" requires retrieval from multiple document sets and comparative reasoning. Sending both through the same pipeline wastes resources on the first and under-serves the second.

Query routing

A router classifies each incoming query and directs it to the appropriate pipeline:

  • Simple factoid: Direct retrieval + generation. Fastest path, lowest cost.
  • Multi-document: Parallel retrieval from multiple indexes, then fusion. Medium complexity.
  • Analytical/comparative: Agentic RAG with multi-step retrieval and reasoning. Slowest but most capable.
  • Out of scope: No retrieval needed. Return a canned response immediately.
  • Structured data: Route to SQL/API instead of vector search. "How many tickets were closed last week?" should query a database, not a document corpus.

Implementing the router

  • LLM-based: Use a fast, cheap LLM (Claude Haiku, GPT-4o-mini) with a classification prompt. Most flexible, ~100ms latency.
  • Classifier-based: Train a lightweight text classifier (distilled BERT) on labeled query types. Faster (~10ms) but requires training data.
  • Rule-based: Pattern matching and keyword heuristics. Fastest but brittle. Good as a first filter before ML-based routing.

Query decomposition

Complex questions that can't be answered with a single retrieval should be decomposed into sub-queries:

Original: "Compare parental leave policies
across our US, UK, and Singapore offices"

Decomposed:
1. "What is the parental leave policy for
    the US office?"
2. "What is the parental leave policy for
    the UK office?"
3. "What is the parental leave policy for
    the Singapore office?"

Each sub-query runs independently, results
are merged for comparative generation.

Step-back prompting

Sometimes the original query is too specific for effective retrieval. Step-back prompting generates a broader version:

  • Original: "Can a part-time contractor in the London office claim dental benefits?"
  • Step-back: "What are the benefit eligibility rules for contractors?"

The broader query retrieves the policy framework. The specific query then filters within that context.

A well-designed router reduces average query cost by 40-60% while improving answer quality on complex queries. Most queries (70-80%) are simple lookups that don't need the full pipeline.
