Recall@K, MRR, and NDCG

Three retrieval metrics every RAG engineer must know

Recall@K

What it measures: Of all the relevant documents that exist, what fraction appears in the top K results?

Recall@K = |relevant docs in top K| / |all relevant docs|

Example: 3 relevant docs exist. Top 10 results
contain 2 of them.
Recall@10 = 2/3 = 0.667

Why it matters for RAG: If the relevant passage isn't in the retrieved set, the LLM can't use it. Recall@K is the ceiling on answer quality. Typical target: Recall@10 > 0.85.

MRR — Mean Reciprocal Rank

What it measures: On average, how high does the first relevant result appear?

RR = 1 / rank_of_first_relevant_result
MRR = average(RR) across all queries

Example: Query 1 → first relevant at rank 1 → RR=1.0
         Query 2 → first relevant at rank 3 → RR=0.33
         Query 3 → first relevant at rank 2 → RR=0.50
         MRR = (1.0 + 0.33 + 0.50) / 3 = 0.61

Why it matters: MRR tells you how quickly users get what they need. An MRR of 0.9+ means the right answer is almost always in position 1.

NDCG@K — Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain

What it measures: How good is the ranking quality, considering that higher positions matter more?

DCG@K  = sum( relevance_i / log2(i+1) ) for i=1..K
IDCG@K = DCG@K with perfect ranking
NDCG@K = DCG@K / IDCG@K

Why it matters: Unlike Recall@K (binary: present or not), NDCG considers the order of results. Two systems might both have Recall@10 = 0.8, but the one with NDCG@10 = 0.9 puts the best results at the top.

Which metric when?

  • Recall@K: Use to evaluate whether your retrieval pipeline finds the right documents at all. The most important metric during development.
  • MRR: Use when you care about the first result (e.g., single-answer questions).
  • NDCG@K: Use when you care about the full ranking (e.g., showing multiple source documents to the user).
