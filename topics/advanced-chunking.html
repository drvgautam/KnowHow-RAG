Advanced Chunking Strategies

Late chunking, contextual retrieval, and parent-child architectures

The problem with naive chunking

Standard chunking splits text at fixed intervals and embeds each chunk independently. This creates two failure modes: (1) context loss — a chunk that says "As mentioned above, the policy allows..." is meaningless in isolation; (2) boundary splits — key information landing exactly on a chunk boundary.

Late chunking

Instead of chunking first and embedding second, late chunking reverses the order:

  1. Embed the entire document through a long-context embedding model
  2. Extract per-token embeddings from the model's hidden states
  3. Pool token embeddings within each chunk boundary to produce chunk embeddings

Each chunk embedding now carries contextual information from the full document, not just from its own tokens. This dramatically improves retrieval for chunks that depend on surrounding context.

Contextual retrieval (Anthropic)

A simpler alternative to late chunking: before embedding each chunk, prepend a short context string generated by an LLM:

Original chunk:
"The deductible is $500 per person per year."

Contextual chunk:
"[From: Employee Benefits Guide, Section 3.2
 - Health Insurance Plan A details]
 The deductible is $500 per person per year."

The prepended context gives the embedding model the "where does this fit" information that naive chunking loses. Anthropic reported 49% fewer retrieval failures with this approach.

Parent-child (hierarchical) chunking

Create two levels of chunks:

  • Child chunks (~200 tokens): Small, precise units used for retrieval. Their embeddings are stored in the vector index.
  • Parent chunks (~800 tokens): Larger context windows. When a child chunk is retrieved, its parent chunk is what gets sent to the LLM.

This gives you precise retrieval (small chunks match specific queries well) with rich context (the LLM sees the broader passage).

Proposition-based chunking

Use an LLM to decompose each document into atomic propositions — self-contained factual statements. Each proposition becomes a chunk:

Document: "Founded in 1998, Acme Corp employs
2,500 people across 12 offices."

Propositions:
1. "Acme Corp was founded in 1998."
2. "Acme Corp employs 2,500 people."
3. "Acme Corp has 12 offices."

Proposition chunks are maximally specific, making them ideal for factoid questions. But the LLM processing cost at indexing time is significant.

The best chunking strategy depends on your query types. Factoid questions favor small, precise chunks. Analytical questions favor large, contextual chunks. Most production systems use hierarchical chunking to serve both.
